## Seizure Types: Definitions and Video Links

### Focal Seizures (Partial Seizures)

Seizures originating in networks limited to one hemisphere. May be discretely localized or more widely distributed.

| Seizure Type | Definition | Video Resource |
|--------------|------------|----------------|
| **Focal Aware Seizure** | Focal seizure with awareness and responsiveness preserved throughout. Previously called "simple partial seizure." | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/seizure/focal-seizure-overview.html) |
| **Focal Impaired Awareness Seizure** | Focal seizure with impaired awareness or responsiveness at any point. Previously called "complex partial seizure." | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/seizure/focal-impaired-awareness-overview.html) |
| **Focal to Bilateral Tonic-Clonic** | Focal seizure that evolves to bilateral convulsive activity. Previously called "secondarily generalized seizure." | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/seizure/focal-bilateral-tonic-clonic-overview.html) |

### Generalized Seizures

Seizures originating at some point within, and rapidly engaging, bilaterally distributed networks.

| Seizure Type | Definition | Video Resource |
|--------------|------------|----------------|
| **Absence Seizure** | Brief (usually <15 seconds) episodes of impaired consciousness with abrupt onset and offset. EEG shows 3 Hz spike-wave discharges. Most common in children ages 4-12. | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/seizure/absence-typical-eeg.html) |
| **Myoclonic Seizure** | Brief, shock-like jerks of a muscle or group of muscles. Usually bilateral. Child remains conscious unless seizure progresses. | [NeoReviews: Myoclonus](https://video.publications.aap.org/neovideos/detail/videos/neurology/video/6196597125001/myoclonus---neonatal-seizures) |
| **Clonic Seizure** | Rhythmic jerking movements involving both sides of the body. More common in infants than older children. | [NeoReviews: Focal Clonic](https://video.publications.aap.org/neovideos/detail/videos/neurology/video/6196591852001/focal-clonic---neonatal-seizures) |
| **Tonic Seizure** | Sustained increase in muscle contraction lasting seconds to minutes. Often occurs during sleep. | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/seizure/tonic-overview.html) |
| **Tonic-Clonic Seizure** | Sequence of tonic phase (stiffening) followed by clonic phase (rhythmic jerking). Previously called "grand mal." | [Boston Children's Hospital](https://www.childrenshospital.org/videos/epilepsy-qa-what-are-major-types-seizures) |
| **Atonic Seizure** | Sudden loss of muscle tone causing head drop or fall ("drop attack"). High injury risk. | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/seizure/atonic-overview.html) |
| **Myoclonic-Atonic Seizure** | Myoclonic jerk followed by atonic component. Characteristic of Doose syndrome. | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/seizure/myoclonic-atonic-overview.html) |

### Neonatal Seizures (Age 0-28 days)

Seizures in neonates have unique semiology due to brain immaturity.

| Seizure Type | Definition | Video Resource |
|--------------|------------|----------------|
| **Clonic (Focal/Multifocal)** | Slow, rhythmic jerking of limb(s) or face. Most easily recognized neonatal seizure type. | [NeoReviews: Focal Clonic](https://video.publications.aap.org/neovideos/detail/videos/neurology/video/6196591852001/focal-clonic---neonatal-seizures) |
| **Tonic (Focal/Generalized)** | Sustained posturing. Focal: asymmetric posturing of limb/trunk. Generalized: decerebrate/decorticate posturing. | [VJNeurology: Neonatal Seizures](https://www.vjneurology.com/video/tabjgcxffy0-diagnosing-neonatal-seizures-clinical-semiology-and-eeg/) |
| **Myoclonic** | Quick, single jerks of limb(s) and trunk. Not rhythmic like clonic seizures. | [NeoReviews: Myoclonus](https://video.publications.aap.org/neovideos/detail/videos/neurology/video/6196597125001/myoclonus---neonatal-seizures) |
| **Epileptic Spasms** | Brief flexor/extensor/mixed spasms, often in clusters. May be subtle in neonates. | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org: West Syndrome](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/syndrome/west-syndrome-overview.html) |
| **Automatisms** | Repetitive stereotyped movements: oral (sucking, chewing), ocular (eye deviation), or limb (pedaling, swimming). | [VJNeurology: ILAE Framework](https://www.vjneurology.com/video/kxi0aosyfak-the-ilae-framework-to-aide-in-determining-the-etiology-of-neonatal-seizures/) |
| **Autonomic** | Apnea, changes in heart rate, blood pressure, skin color, pupil size, or salivation. | [VJNeurology: Neonatal Classification](https://www.vjneurology.com/video/unliipcucew-updated-guidelines-for-the-classification-of-neonatal-epilepsy-syndromes/) |

### Infantile Seizures (Age 1-24 months)

| Seizure Type | Definition | Video Resource |
|--------------|------------|----------------|
| **Epileptic (Infantile) Spasms** | Brief (1-2 second) flexor, extensor, or mixed spasms, typically occurring in clusters upon awakening. Associated with developmental regression. Core feature of Infantile Epileptic Spasms Syndrome (IESS/West Syndrome). | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/syndrome/west-syndrome-overview.html) |
| **Febrile Seizures** | Seizures occurring with fever (>38Â°C) in children 6 months to 5 years without CNS infection. Simple (<15 min, generalized) vs Complex (>15 min, focal, or recurrent within 24h). | [Child Neurology Foundation](https://www.childneurologyfoundation.org/epilepsy-parent-education-videos/) |

### Pediatric Epilepsy Syndromes with Characteristic Seizure Types

| Syndrome | Age of Onset | Characteristic Seizures | Video Resource |
|----------|--------------|------------------------|----------------|
| **Infantile Epileptic Spasms Syndrome (West)** | 3-12 months | Epileptic spasms in clusters, hypsarrhythmia on EEG | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/syndrome/west-syndrome-overview.html) |
| **Dravet Syndrome** | ~6 months | Prolonged febrile hemiconvulsive seizures, later multiple seizure types | [NeurologyLive Video Series](https://www.neurologylive.com/peers-perspectives/rare-childhood-epilepsies-dravet-syndrome-and-lennox-gastaut-syndrome) |
| **Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome** | 1-7 years | Tonic, atonic, atypical absence; slow spike-wave on EEG | [NeurologyLive Video Series](https://www.neurologylive.com/peers-perspectives/rare-childhood-epilepsies-dravet-syndrome-and-lennox-gastaut-syndrome) |
| **Childhood Absence Epilepsy** | 4-10 years | Typical absence seizures, 3 Hz spike-wave | [EpilepsyDiagnosis.org](https://www.epilepsydiagnosis.org/syndrome/cae-eeg.html) |
| **Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy** | 12-18 years | Myoclonic jerks (especially morning), generalized tonic-clonic | [CURE Epilepsy](https://www.cureepilepsy.org/epilepsy-explained/juvenile_myoclonic_epilepsy_explained/) |
| **Epilepsy with Myoclonic Absences** | 1-12 years (peak ~7) | Absence with rhythmic myoclonic jerks of shoulders/arms | [MedLink Neurology](https://www.medlink.com/media/88466138) |
